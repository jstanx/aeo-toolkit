# Evidence: Before / After

Worked examples for `evidence-and-citations`. Each shows weak copy that engines discount
and the evidence-backed version they prefer to cite.

## Example 1 — a vague claim becomes a sourced statistic

**Before (unsupported):**
> Our onboarding is fast and most teams love it.

Problem: "fast" and "most" are unverifiable; an engine has nothing to lift or trust.

**After (specific + sourced):**
> Teams reach their first tracked event in a median of 4 minutes, and 63% complete setup
> on day one (2026 onboarding study of 1,200 teams).

Why it works: a concrete, attributed number is quotable, checkable, and citation-worthy.

## Example 2 — a superlative becomes attributed proof

**Before (hype):**
> We're the leading analytics platform for SaaS.

Problem: an unbacked superlative reads as marketing and gets deprioritized.

**After (third-party attribution):**
> Rated the top-scoring product analytics tool for B2B SaaS in G2's Winter 2026 report,
> based on 480 verified reviews.

Why it works: the claim now rests on an independent, nameable source.

## Example 3 — an assertion gains an inline citation

**Before (assertion):**
> Recently updated content gets cited far more often by AI.

**After (cited):**
> Recently updated content is cited substantially more by answer engines — studies of AI
> citations report meaningfully higher inclusion for pages refreshed within ~30 days
> ([source]). 

Why it works: linking the claim to an authoritative source raises the page's own odds of
being cited, and is especially helpful for sites that are not yet top authorities.

## Pattern

For every checkable claim, ask: *what's the number, the source, or the named voice behind
this?* Attach it, or soften the claim. Aim for a concrete, sourced point every few
paragraphs — enough to be verifiable, not so much it reads as noise. Keep figures accurate
and current; a wrong or stale statistic costs more trust than a missing one.
